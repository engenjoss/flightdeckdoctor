Pilot Medical FAQ (Canada)

Clear answers to the most common questions about pilot medical requirements in Canada, based on Transport Canada guidelines.

What medical do I need to be a pilot in Canada?

In Canada, the type of pilot medical you need depends on the licence you are pursuing under Transport Canada regulations.

  • Category 1: Required for commercial and airline pilots
  • Category 3: Required for private pilots
  • Category 4: Used for recreational flying

For a full breakdown, see our medical categories page.

How long does a Cat 1 medical last?

It depends on your age.

  • Under 40 - 12 Months
  • Over 40 - 6 Months

For a complete breakdown of validity periods, see our medical categories page.

I had my medical submitted and later I got a letter from TC saying that my medical was being renewed. What does this mean?

This does not necessarily mean that anything is wrong with your medical application. They may just need extra time to review your file as TC is currently experiencing staffing issues.

It may also mean that their system has flagged a potential issue and they need time to review the medical.

I have Sleep Apnea, can I still fly in Canada?

Typically Transport Canada requires a lengthy process before they will clear you to fly. There is no cookie cutter answer, everyone is different, but the following process may be needed:

  • Undergo a sleep study
  • Be prescribed a CPAP or other treatment device
  • Follow-up sleep study after treatment begins
  • Demonstrate treatment effectiveness and compliance
  • Transport Canada medical review of results

For a complete breakdown of Sleep Apnea, see our Medical Conditions Page and look in the section "Mental".

Can I fly in Canada after surgery?

Pilots are generally not permitted to fly immediately after surgery. Certification may be restored once recovery is complete and the pilot is no longer at risk of impairment.

For a complete breakdown of Sleep Apnea, see our Medical Conditions Page and look in the section "Surgery".

Can Canadian Pilots get LASIK Surgery?

LASIK and other refractive eye surgeries are commonly used to correct vision. According to Transport Canada, these procedures are considered elective and may be acceptable for pilots if proper recovery and evaluation requirements are met.

I have bad vision, can I still fly in Canada?

Yes, many pilots with vision impairments can still fly with corrective lenses. Transport Canada allows for glasses or contact lenses to meet vision standards, but certain conditions may require additional evaluation.

For a complete breakdown of Vision impairments, see our Medical Conditions Page and look in the section "Vision".
