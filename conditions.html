Medical Conditions

Important Notice

This page provides general guidance only. Aviation medical decisions are made on a case-by-case basis by Transport Canada and a CAME.

How Medical Conditions Are Assessed

Transport Canada does not automatically disqualify applicants based on a diagnosis. Instead, decisions are based on risk of incapacitation, stability of the condition, and supporting medical evidence.

Cardiovascular Conditions

Conditions related to the heart and blood vessels.

High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)

Overview

High blood pressure is a common condition where the force of blood against the artery walls is elevated.

Can You Fly?

Pilots may be certified if blood pressure is stable and well-controlled.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Blood pressure readings over time
  • Medication use and side effects
  • Risk of cardiovascular complications

Possible Requirements

  • Regular blood pressure monitoring
  • Physician reports
  • Possible ECG testing

Pilot Tips

Lifestyle changes such as diet, exercise, and stress management can improve outcomes and simplify medical approval.

Metabolic Conditions

Conditions affecting metabolism and blood chemistry.

Diabetes

Overview

Diabetes is a condition affecting blood sugar regulation, ranging from mild to insulin-dependent forms.

Can You Fly?

Pilots may be certified if the condition is stable and well-controlled.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Stability of blood sugar levels
  • Risk of hypoglycemia
  • Presence of complications
  • Consistency of monitoring

Possible Requirements

  • Blood test results (e.g. A1c)
  • Glucose monitoring records
  • Specialist reports
  • Regular follow-ups

Pilot Reality

Expect additional documentation and ongoing monitoring. Approval is often possible with consistent management.

Mental Health Conditions

Conditions affecting mood, behavior, and cognitive function.

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Overview

ADHD affects attention, focus, and impulse control. It may be managed with lifestyle strategies or medication.

Can You Fly?

Active ADHD or current use of ADHD medication is generally disqualifying. Eligibility may exist if off medication and symptom-free for 6+ months.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Current diagnostic status and symptom history
  • Medication use (stimulants or non-stimulants)
  • Neuropsychological testing confirming stability
  • Risk of attention lapses impacting flight safety

Possible Requirements

  • Detailed medical and psychiatric history
  • Specialist or neuropsychological reports
  • Evidence of sustained symptom control without medication
  • Ongoing monitoring if cleared

Pilot Tips

Maintain thorough documentation and consult a CAME early. Early disclosure improves likelihood of certification.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

Overview

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) is a condition where breathing repeatedly stops and starts during sleep, leading to poor sleep quality and daytime fatigue.

Can You Fly?

Yes—pilots can often be certified with sleep apnea if the condition is properly treated and controlled. Untreated OSA is a concern due to the risk of fatigue and impaired alertness.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Severity of the condition (mild, moderate, severe)
  • Compliance with treatment (e.g. CPAP usage)
  • Daytime alertness and fatigue levels
  • Sleep study results

Possible Requirements

  • Sleep study (polysomnography)
  • CPAP compliance reports
  • Specialist (sleep physician) reports
  • Regular follow-up assessments

Pilot Reality

Many pilots continue flying with sleep apnea once effectively treated. Consistent CPAP use and proper documentation are key to maintaining medical certification.

Musculoskeletal Conditions

Conditions affecting bones, joints, and physical mobility.

Broken Bones (or Fractures)

Overview

Broken bones (fractures) are common injuries that may temporarily affect a pilot’s ability to meet medical requirements, depending on severity and recovery.

Can You Fly?

Pilots are generally not permitted to fly while a fracture is healing if it limits mobility or function. Certification is typically restored once the injury has healed and normal function has returned.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Location and severity of the fracture
  • Healing progress and stability
  • Range of motion and strength
  • Risk of complications or re-injury

Possible Requirements

  • Physician clearance confirming healing
  • Medical reports or imaging (if required)
  • Functional assessment (mobility and strength)
  • Temporary grounding during recovery

Pilot Reality

Most pilots return to flying after a fracture once fully healed. The key factor is demonstrating safe physical function with no significant limitations to your CAME.

Surgical Procedures

Medical procedures that may temporarily affect pilot fitness and certification.

General Surgery

Overview

Surgical procedures can impact a pilot’s medical certification depending on the type of surgery, recovery time, and any ongoing limitations or complications.

Can You Fly?

Pilots are generally not permitted to fly immediately after surgery. Certification may be restored once recovery is complete and the pilot is no longer at risk of impairment.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Type and complexity of the surgery
  • Recovery status and healing progress
  • Presence of complications or side effects
  • Use of medications that may affect performance

Possible Requirements

  • Surgeon or physician clearance
  • Post-operative medical reports
  • Proof of full recovery and stable condition
  • Temporary grounding during recovery period

Pilot Reality

Many pilots return to flying after surgery once fully recovered. The key is demonstrating that there are no functional limitations, complications, or medication effects that could impact safety.

LASIK Surgery

Overview

LASIK and other refractive eye surgeries are commonly used to correct vision. According to Transport Canada, these procedures are considered elective and may be acceptable for pilots if proper recovery and evaluation requirements are met.

Can You Fly?

Yes—pilots may be certified after LASIK, but they must be temporarily grounded until Transport Canada reviews the results of the surgery and confirms that it is safe to return to flying.

What Transport Canada Looks At

  • Stability of vision after surgery
  • Absence of side effects such as glare, halos, or reduced contrast sensitivity
  • Final visual acuity meeting required standards
  • Type of procedure performed

Possible Requirements

  • Mandatory reporting of the procedure to a CAME
  • Post-surgery medical report (typically around 30 days after surgery)
  • Assessment by an optometrist or ophthalmologist
  • Temporary grounding until approval is granted

Pilot Reality

Many pilots successfully return to flying after LASIK. However, approval depends on demonstrating stable vision and no complications that could affect flight safety, particularly night vision issues such as glare or halos.
